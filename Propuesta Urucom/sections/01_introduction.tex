\section{Introduction}

Multichannel surface-wave methods use distributed vibration measurements to estimate Rayleigh-wave dispersion and infer near-surface mechanical properties \cite{Park1999,Foti2014}. Although their processing is well established, acquisition hardware remains a major source of cost and deployment complexity. Low-cost geophone acquisition has been demonstrated with embedded mixed-signal hardware \cite{Kafadar2020}, and embedded geophone interfaces combine instrumentation amplification, filtering, and resistor-adjustable gain \cite{Wijayaraja2024}. Achieving low offset through higher-grade analog components, however, raises the cost of every node. This work instead uses resources already present in the microcontroller to correct the operating point of a budget-constrained front end.

The system belongs to a low-cost, reconfigurable Internet-of-Things acquisition platform \cite{Guevara2015} based on SM-24 moving-coil geophones \cite{SM24}. Its programmable system-on-chip (PSoC) 5LP integrates programmable-gain amplifiers (PGAs), operational amplifiers, analog filters, voltage digital-to-analog converters (VDACs), multiplexers, a delta-sigma analog-to-digital converter (ADC), a digital filter, direct memory access (DMA), and an Arm Cortex-M3 processor \cite{InfineonPSoC}. Time-sharing the ADC and digital filter between acquisition and calibration avoids a separate converter or processor, following digitally assisted analog design \cite{Murmann2006}. The tradeoff is that the internal analog blocks cannot be independently selected to optimize noise and offset, a limitation also reported for a single-chip PSoC potentiostat \cite{Lopin2018}.

The conditioning circuit derives from the cascaded geophone front end of Ma \emph{et al.} \cite{Ma2023}. On the PSoC, accumulated DC errors consumed operating range at intermediate and final nodes; a finite foreground calibration recenters them because a continuous servo proved impractical (Section~\ref{sec:discussion}).

Prior PSoC calibration estimated and stored numerical transimpedance-amplifier, PGA, and ADC corrections under internal stimulus \cite{Lopin2018,InfineonAN68403}, while body-bias calibration physically trimmed one amplifier \cite{Mohan2020}. Here, the loop instead adjusts the physical reference of each trim-accessible node within the available VDAC range and resolution.

The sensor-to-PGA and direct-SUM paths remain DC-coupled because placing a blocking corner ahead of the instrumentation input well below 1~Hz would require a large-value off-chip coupling capacitor. Per-stage trimming addresses offset without introducing that component or classical autozero/chopper circuitry \cite{EnzTemes1996}.

The objective is to establish the feasibility of recentering accumulated DC offsets with PSoC-resident sensing, filtering, and VDAC actuation, without added calibration circuitry, and to quantify preliminary performance against a shared fixed-reference baseline rather than per-condition optimized codes. The contributions are: 1) a four-VDAC per-stage architecture reusing the PSoC multiplexer, ADC, and digital filter; 2) a bounded deadband controller that freezes its codes before acquisition; and 3) a two-board proof-of-concept evaluation over PGA settings $\times1$--$\times50$ with one controller-parameter set. Sections~\ref{sec:platform-design}--\ref{sec:self-calibration} present the architecture and controller, Section~\ref{sec:experimental-validation} the experiments, and Sections~\ref{sec:discussion} and~VI the interpretation and conclusions.
