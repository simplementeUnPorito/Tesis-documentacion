\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

The method trades component precision for system-level observability and digital assistance. Its benefit is not that the ADC output has zero mean -- a software subtraction could do that -- but that the DACs modify the physical operating points before large downstream gains apply, so the correction is agnostic to the error source, provided the stage is observable and the DAC has sufficient authority and range.

The foreground method was adopted after a continuously active DC servo showed a low corner centered impractically slowly, a faster loop interfered with the signal, and a gain change forced another transient. The proposed controller instead disconnects, holding DAC outputs constant throughout acquisition, adding neither time-varying feedback nor extra servo poles; EEPROM records avoid the full transient after most gain changes. Since the DC estimator is an aggressive low-pass filter, transient seismic energy is itself attenuated, so the method should tolerate a non-silent site given no sustained disturbance during convergence, though this was not validated under controlled vibration. The method corrects the offset present when calibration runs, not drift directly; recalibrating at boot, gain change, or on command (Section~III) limits drift accumulation, though temperature/time-resolved drift was not characterized. Remaining costs are finite DAC resolution and an inability to correct noise, distortion, or an exhausted range.

The controller is implemented generically as PI, but in practice runs as a pure integrator: with the proportional gains of Table~\ref{tab:cal_params}, the P-term's maximum contribution stays under one hundredth of a DAC LSB for any error the ADC can resolve. This is a deliberate choice, not a shortfall -- response speed is not a priority for this application, since calibration triggers once after deployment and a one-time delay is acceptable, so no attempt was made to speed up the response with a larger $k_p$. A full four-stage run completes in approximately 10~s typical (Fig.~\ref{fig:calibration_trace}); the per-stage timeouts of Table~\ref{tab:cal_params} sum to 57.6~s worst case, reached only if every stage times out, which was not observed in the runs reported here. A faster controller could replace the current law without changing the observation and correction hardware, should a future application need it.

Two independently assembled boards were characterized across the full gain range for this work; the headroom result of Table~\ref{tab:results} holds on both despite the two boards differing markedly in their default (uncalibrated) operating point (Section~IV), evidence the method is not tuned to one particular assembly. Repeated runs on both boards (Section~IV) begin to separate algorithmic repeatability from board-to-board variation; a larger population and cross-temperature trials would be needed to generalize further. As noted in Section~I, the comparison with prior self-calibration work \cite{Lopin2018,InfineonAN68403} is architectural, not quantitative, since those methods target the ADC transfer characteristic rather than each stage's physical operating point.
