\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

The method trades component precision for system-level measurement and digital assistance, unlike precision front ends characterized near the sensor's Johnson-noise limit \cite{Kirchhoff2017}. Its benefit is not that the ADC output has zero mean -- a software subtraction could do that -- but that the DACs modify the physical operating points before large downstream gains apply, so the correction is agnostic to the error source, provided the stage is measurable and the DAC has sufficient authority and range.

The foreground method was adopted after a continuously active DC servo showed a low corner centered impractically slowly, a faster loop interfered with the signal, and a gain change forced another transient -- the corner-frequency/settling tradeoff reported for DC-servo offset compensation \cite{Liu2020}. The proposed controller instead disconnects, holding DAC outputs constant throughout acquisition, adding no time-varying feedback or servo poles; EEPROM avoids the full transient after most gain changes. The method corrects the offset present when calibration runs, not drift directly; recalibrating at boot, gain change, or on command (Section~\ref{sec:self-calibration}) limits drift accumulation. Remaining limitations are finite DAC resolution, no correction of noise or distortion, and unvalidated behavior under sustained vibration or thermal drift during convergence.

The controller runs as a pure integrator ($k_{p,i}=0$ in Eq.~\ref{eq:pi}), not a shortfall: response speed is not a priority for a calibration that triggers once after deployment, so a one-time delay is acceptable. A full four-stage run completes in approximately 10~s typical (Fig.~\ref{fig:calibration_trace}); the per-stage timeouts of Table~\ref{tab:cal_params} sum to 57.6~s worst case, reached only if every stage times out, which was not observed in the runs reported here.

Two independently assembled boards were characterized across the full gain range for this work; the headroom result of Table~\ref{tab:results} holds on both despite markedly different default operating points, evidence the method is not tuned to one particular assembly. Repeated runs on both boards (Section~IV) begin to separate algorithmic repeatability from board-to-board variation; a larger population and cross-temperature trials would be needed to generalize further. As noted in Section~I, the comparison with prior self-calibration work \cite{Lopin2018,InfineonAN68403} is architectural, not quantitative, since those methods target the ADC transfer characteristic rather than each stage's physical operating point.
