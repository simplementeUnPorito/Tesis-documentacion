\begin{abstract}
This paper presents a foreground calibration method for a low-cost, cascaded geophone analog front end, where amplifier and reference offsets compound stage-to-stage and can drive intermediate nodes into saturation. The proposed method sequentially observes and independently trims four cascaded stages -- a programmable-gain amplifier (PGA), a band-pass filter (BP), a summing stage (SUM), and a low-pass filter (LP) -- reusing existing on-chip conversion and filtering resources rather than adding dedicated calibration hardware. Oscilloscope measurements on two independently assembled prototype boards, across the PGA's full configurable gain range ($\times1$ to $\times50$), show that after calibration the stage nearest the ADC is left within 4\% of the ADC's full-scale range in every board/gain combination tested, down from as much as 46\% uncalibrated. The PGA stage converges only partially under some conditions -- shown to result from the loop's deadband gating convergence at that stage specifically, not from a hardware limit -- but this does not affect the headroom result, which is measured after the full cascade and dominated by the downstream stages. A full four-stage run completes in approximately 10~s typical (57.6~s worst case). The calibrated front end has additionally produced coherent multichannel surface-wave field records, demonstrating operation beyond the bench.
\end{abstract}
