\begin{abstract}
This paper presents a digitally assisted foreground calibration method for a low-cost geophone analog front end implemented in a PSoC~5LP mixed-signal system-on-chip. Cascaded gain stages in the front end multiply the offset and drift of the on-chip operational amplifiers, reducing the available dynamic range and eventually driving intermediate nodes into saturation; a fixed correction at the summing node is functional but cannot restore the operating points of the remaining stages. The proposed method sequentially observes four stages -- a programmable-gain amplifier (PGA), a band-pass filter (BP), a summing stage (ADDER), and a low-pass filter (LP) -- through an on-chip analog multiplexer, reusing the same delta-sigma analog-to-digital converter (ADC) already used for geophone acquisition together with an otherwise-idle digital filter block reconfigured to estimate each stage's DC component. A discrete-time controller with deadband and anti-windup, implemented generically as PI but run in practice as a pure integrator since response speed is not a priority for this application, adjusts four independent voltage DACs (VDACs) until every stage output approaches its target operating point; neither the controller law nor the DAC realization is intrinsic to the architecture. Once converged, the DAC codes are frozen for acquisition and stored per programmable-gain setting. Oscilloscope measurements on two independently assembled prototype boards, across the PGA's full configurable gain range ($\times1$ to $\times50$), show that after calibration the stage nearest the ADC is left within 4\% of the ADC's full-scale range in every board/gain combination tested, down from as much as 46\% uncalibrated. The PGA stage converges only partially under some conditions -- shown to result from the loop's deadband gating convergence at that stage specifically, not from a hardware limit -- but this does not affect the headroom result, which is measured after the full cascade and dominated by the downstream stages. A full four-stage run completes in approximately 10~s typical (57.6~s worst case). The calibrated front end has additionally produced coherent multichannel surface-wave field records, demonstrating operation beyond the bench.
\end{abstract}
