\section{Experimental Validation}

\subsection{Prototype and Comparison Conditions}

The validation compares two configurations on two independently assembled SM-24 geophone-conditioning prototypes (Board~A, Board~B), each containing the four independently controlled VDAC references: \emph{Default}, fixed nominal VDAC references without calibration, and \emph{Proposed}, sequential four-stage calibration followed by frozen-reference acquisition. A third prototype was built but is not included in this quantitative comparison.

The earlier ADDER-only workaround (Section~\ref{sec:platform-design}) is not re-measured here: it was already characterized across five prior prototypes with mediocre results, uncompensated drift up to 1~V on the worst boards, because it only recenters the ADDER node and leaves the pre- and post-ADDER stages free to drift independently. Measurements span the PGA's full configurable gain range, from $\times1$ to $\times50$, rather than a single representative setting, so that the claim of board- and gain-independent operation is tested directly instead of assumed from one operating point.

\subsection{Design Target: Headroom, Not Zero}

The calibration loop is not designed to null every stage's offset exactly. Its deadband, timeout, and controller gains are firmware parameters, not physical constants (Section~III): a tighter deadband is harder to reach and easier to leave, a longer FIR estimation window improves the DC estimate at the cost of convergence time, and a longer per-stage timeout lets a conservative controller finish without needing to be aggressive. The design target that these parameters serve is that no stage, on any board, at any gain, leaves the signal at risk of railing the ADC -- not that every stage converges to zero with equal precision. Section~\ref{sec:pga-note} shows a case where this target is met even though one individual stage converges only partially.

\subsection{Measured Headroom Recovery Across the Gain Range}

For each board and gain, the DC offset at the output of the PGA, BP, ADDER, and LP stages was measured with a Tektronix TDS~2004B oscilloscope (4~channel, 60~MHz, 1~GS/s), reading the mean voltage over a 100~ms window after the transient settled, both with the default (uncalibrated) VDAC references and after the proposed calibration converged. Table~\ref{tab:results} reports the offset remaining at the LP stage -- the one immediately upstream of the ADC, and therefore the one that determines whether the signal rails -- as a percentage of the ADC's $\pm2.5$~V full-scale range, $N{=}10$ independent runs per board/gain cell. Board~B was not exercised at $\times1$ and $\times4$: at those gain/board combinations the default offset already falls inside the loop's normal deadband, so the controller correctly reports convergence without acting, and forcing it to run required lowering the deadband by hand, which changes the test condition (Section~\ref{sec:pga-note}).

Across every board/gain combination measured, calibration leaves the LP stage at $\leq$4\% of the ADC's full-scale range, down from as much as 46\% uncalibrated on Board~A at $\times1$. Board~A's default headroom consumption falls as gain increases (its default state is closer to the rail at low gain than at high gain), while Board~B starts, and stays, much closer to zero across the whole range -- the two boards differ in their unpowered/default operating point, yet calibration brings both into the same narrow band. This is the behavior the design targets: regardless of the board or the starting offset, the calibrated front end lands within a small, consistent fraction of the ADC range.

\subsection{The PGA Stage: A Partial Result, Explained}
\label{sec:pga-note}

Unlike BP, ADDER, and LP, the PGA stage's post-calibration offset is not consistently much smaller than Default: across the tested conditions, calibration reduces the mean $|\text{offset}|$ at the PGA by roughly 4--54\%, with the smallest reductions at $\times1$ and $\times50$. Two effects contribute. First, the deadband $\Delta_i$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:scale}) is a firmware margin, not a physical limit: on Board~B at $\times4$, the default PGA offset fell inside the normal deadband on every run attempted, and the controller declared convergence immediately without moving the DAC -- observed directly, not inferred -- until the deadband was manually tightened. This confirms that a wide-enough deadband can gate convergence at the PGA stage specifically. Second, the FIR window used to estimate the DC level (128 samples) sets a floor on how quickly a small residual can be resolved from noise; a longer window would improve the estimate at the cost of convergence time, a tradeoff left for future tuning rather than explored here. Both explanations point to the same place: the PGA's limitation is a choice of firmware parameters, not a hardware ceiling, and it does not compromise the headroom target in Table~\ref{tab:results}, which is measured after the full four-stage cascade and is dominated by the LP stage, not the PGA.

Fig.~\ref{fig:calibration_trace} shows an oscilloscope capture of the four observed stage outputs during an actual run, spanning the sequential PGA~$\rightarrow$~BP~$\rightarrow$~ADDER~$\rightarrow$~LP correction. Each output steps as the controller reaches it and holds once converged, visible as the successive plateaus in the ADDER and LP traces; the full sequence completes in approximately 10~s.
